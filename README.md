# Foundations

**Foundations of cohesive thinking:** exploring how ideas, structures, and systems bind, compose, scale, and transform—viewed from multiple perspectives to reveal invariants and shared patterns across mathematics, computation, visualization, and abstraction.

---

## Overview

`foundations` is a collection of mathematical, computational, and conceptual explorations focused on understanding ideas as **cohesive structures**.

The goal is not only to study isolated topics, but to understand how different structures relate, compose, transform, and reveal common patterns across domains.

A function, a graph, a sequence, a vector space, a pipeline, a category, a matrix, a tensor, a system, or even a mental model can often be seen as different expressions of deeper structural principles.

This repository explores those principles.

---

## What is Cohesian?

**Cohesian** is a way of thinking centered around cohesion.

It looks at knowledge through structure, relation, composition, transformation, invariants, and perspective. Instead of treating ideas as separate fragments, Cohesian tries to understand how they connect, overlap, scale, transform, and fit together.

It is inspired by patterns found across mathematics, computation, systems, visual reasoning, and structural abstraction.

Some recurring questions are:

- How do ideas compose?
- What remains invariant when a structure is transformed?
- How can the same concept be seen from different perspectives?
- When do different areas share the same underlying structure?
- How do small components bind into larger systems?
- How can visual, algebraic, discrete, and computational models describe the same thing?
- What makes a structure flexible, reusable, and scalable?

Cohesian is not limited to one field.

It is a lens for seeing relationships between fields.

This repository contains foundational explorations behind Cohesian thinking.

It includes papers, notes, and executable notebooks about mathematical structures, abstractions, and models. Many ideas are written first as Markdown papers and then extended into interactive notebooks when computation, visualization, or experimentation can make the idea clearer.

Jupyter notebooks provide a way to experiment, visualize, test examples, and interact with the ideas.

## Core themes

### Cohesion

How separate ideas, objects, structures, or systems connect into something meaningful.

Cohesion is the central idea: the hidden fit between parts.

### Structure

The form beneath the surface.

A structure may appear as a graph, a function, a matrix, a sequence, a pipeline, a category, a vector space, a tensor, or a visual model.

Different representations can often reveal the same underlying pattern.

### Composition

How simple parts combine into larger systems.

Composition appears in functions, wrappers, pipelines, interfaces, graphs, algebraic structures, software design, category theory, and many other areas.

### Binding

How parts hold together.

A binder may appear as an interface, a relation, a contract, an edge, a mapping, a constraint, a wrapper, a transformation, or a shared invariant.

Binding is what lets structures scale.

### Transformation

How structures change while preserving something important.

Transformations help reveal invariants, symmetries, equivalent views, and deeper relationships between representations.

### Perspective

The same idea can often be seen in multiple ways.

A concept may have an algebraic view, a geometric view, a graph view, a computational view, a categorical view, a visual view, or a systems view.

Cohesian thinking tries to move between those views.

### Invariants

What remains stable across change.

Invariants are central because they reveal what is essential. When a structure changes representation, scale, or context, invariants help identify what is still the same.

### Shared patterns across domains

Different fields often reuse the same structural ideas.

Graphs, sets, functions, transformations, spaces, composition, constraints, relations, mappings, matrices, and abstractions appear everywhere.

This repository studies those patterns across areas instead of keeping them isolated.

## Reading philosophy

Each exploration can be read in two ways.

### As a paper

The Markdown version is meant to be readable without running code.

It should explain the main idea, motivation, definitions, examples, perspectives, invariants, and conclusions.

### As an executable notebook

The notebook version is meant to make the idea interactive.

It may include code, diagrams, plots, experiments, examples, counterexamples, simulations, or visual explanations.

The notebook is not only an implementation detail.

It is part of the thinking process.

## Design principles

### Clarity before complexity

Ideas should be explained from their simplest meaningful form before adding abstraction.

### Structure before syntax

The important part is the structure behind the notation, not the notation itself.

Notation should reveal the idea, not hide it.

### Multiple views are better than one

A strong idea should be understandable from different perspectives.

When possible, explorations should include several views: symbolic, visual, computational, geometric, discrete, categorical, or structural.

### Invariants matter

When changing perspective, representation, or scale, the key question is:

> What stays the same?

### Composition is fundamental

Small structures should be understandable as parts of larger systems.

A good abstraction should be composable.

### Visual thinking is part of reasoning

Diagrams, graphs, plots, and visual models are not decorations.

They are tools for thought.

### Computation can clarify theory

Code is used to test, visualize, and interact with ideas.

A notebook can reveal structure that is hard to see in static text.
