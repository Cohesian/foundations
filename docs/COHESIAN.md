# Cohesian

**Cohesian** is a way of thinking centered on cohesion: how ideas connect through structure, relation, composition, transformation, invariants, and perspective — across mathematics, computation, systems, and visual reasoning.

It is not limited to one field. It is a lens for seeing relationships between fields.

---

## Recurring questions

- How do ideas compose?
- What remains invariant when a structure is transformed?
- How can the same concept be seen from different perspectives?
- When do different areas share the same underlying structure?
- How do small components bind into larger systems?
- How can visual, algebraic, discrete, and computational models describe the same thing?
- What makes a structure flexible, reusable, and scalable?

---

## Core themes

### Cohesion

How separate ideas, objects, structures, or systems connect into something meaningful — the hidden fit between parts.

### Structure

The form beneath the surface: a graph, function, matrix, sequence, pipeline, category, vector space, tensor, or visual model. Different representations often reveal the same underlying pattern.

### Composition

How simple parts combine into larger systems — in functions, wrappers, pipelines, interfaces, graphs, algebra, software design, category theory, and beyond.

### Binding

How parts hold together: an interface, relation, contract, edge, mapping, constraint, wrapper, transformation, or shared invariant. Binding is what lets structures scale.

### Transformation

How structures change while preserving something important — revealing invariants, symmetries, equivalent views, and relationships between representations.

### Perspective

The same idea in algebraic, geometric, graph, computational, categorical, visual, or systems form. Cohesian thinking moves between those views.

### Invariants

What stays stable across change — essential when representation, scale, or context shifts.

### Shared patterns across domains

Graphs, sets, functions, transformations, spaces, composition, constraints, relations, mappings, matrices, and abstractions recur everywhere. This k-graph studies them across areas instead of in isolation.
